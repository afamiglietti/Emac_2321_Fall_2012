		<p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0857685813/boingboing">The Twenty-Year Death</a> by Ariel S. Winter is a 700-page hardbound crime novel that's written in the form of three separate crime novels (which are all tied together). The 1931 novel is in the style of Georges Simenon. The 1941 novel is in the style of Raymond Chandler, and the 1951 novel is in the style of Jim Thompson. I'm looking forward to reading this!</p>

<p>Below, an essay by the author that explains how he conceived of the novel. Following that, three short excerpts from each novel.</p>

<blockquote><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0857685813/boingboing"><img src="http://boingboing.net/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/twentyyeardeath.jpg" alt="Twentyyeardeath" title="twentyyeardeath.jpg" border="0" width="300" height="480" align = "right" /></a>In the summer of 2007, I moved back to Baltimore after a year&rsquo;s sojourn in New York City. I was in the grips of a crippling depression, spending my days immobile on the couch with a book, except for the two or three hours I managed to write at the library. </p>

<p>One of the books I read at that time was <i>Austerlitz</i> by W. G. Sebald, and I was once again enamored with Sebald&rsquo;s first person narrator, a precise, ruminative stand-in for Sebald himself. I wanted to do something like it, so I began a book about an anguished, brooding man reading books on a couch. Sounds exciting, right? </p>

<p>In this book, the books the narrator read were going to appear in full: a mystery, a romance, a western, a history, a biography, sci-fi, everything. I described it as David Mitchell&rsquo;s <i>Cloud Atlas</i> (another of my favorite books) as written by W. G. Sebald. </p>

<p>The first book I wrote within the frame narrative was a Georges Simenon pastiche set in 1931, <i>Malniveau Prison</i>, simply because I was reading a lot of Simenon during my long days on the couch. I moved on to the second book -- a quirky romance of sorts -- but I was quickly weighed down by the enormous task I had set myself, and abandoned the whole project. I knew, however, that <i>Malniveau Prison</i> was still quite good, so I sent it off to an agent. He was encouraging, pushing me to expand <i>Malniveau Prison</i> to a full-length book on its own, which I endeavored to do. As I was writing, I began to think: wouldn&rsquo;t it be interesting if there were a series of mysteries in which, rather than following the detective from book to book, we followed a secondary character? And since I was writing in the style of one great crime writer, wouldn&rsquo;t it be interesting if each of those novels was in the style of a different crime novelist? One of the characters in <i>Malniveau Prison</i> was an American writer, and it seemed natural that an American writer in France would next move to Raymond Chandler&rsquo;s Hollywood of the 1940s. And if there were ten years between Simenon and Chandler, there should be a similar jump between the second and third books, and who was the premier crime writer of the 1950s? Jim Thompson. So, with the whole plan now before me, I began to re-read those authors over and over and over, and started to write what became <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0857685813/boingboing"><i>The Twenty-Year Death</i></a>.</p></blockquote>
